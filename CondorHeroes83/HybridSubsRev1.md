# HybridSubsRev1
**Hybrid subtitle revision rules — Legend of the Condor Heroes (射鵰英雄傳)**
Derived from parallel Mandarin Chinese / Cantonese corpus analysis.

---

## Rule 1 — Proper nouns → Chinese characters
All proper nouns (people, places, organisations, technique names, epithets) are written in Chinese characters, using the Mandarin as authoritative. Romanised forms from the English source are replaced.

Examples:
- Guo Xiaotian → 郭嘯天
- Niu Family Village → 牛家村
- Lotus Pond Village → 荷塘村
- My surname is Mu → My surname is 穆
- East Heretic → 東邪
- Wanyan Kang (romanised) → 完顏康

---

## Rule 2 — Honorific address forms → Chinese characters
Address forms and relational honorifics are kept in Chinese. This includes:
- Kinship titles used as address: 娘, 爹, 父王
- Martial titles: 師父, 師叔, 師兄, 道長, 上人
- Social titles: 王爺, 王妃, 少爺, 公子, 姑娘, 大叔, 世伯
- Pet names / diminutives: 蓉兒, 靖兒, 康兒

Examples:
- Teacher / Master (address) → 師父
- Fifth Uncle (martial) → 五師叔
- Taoist Qiu → 丘道長
- Rong-er → 蓉兒
- Mother → 娘
- Your Highness (prince) → 王爺

---

## Rule 3 — Pronouns & self-introductions → English
First-person self-referential forms are translated to natural English. Humble or formal self-references (貧道, 在下, 弟子, 徒兒, 孩兒, 小生, 小女, 為師 etc.) become "I", "me", "my" etc. Self-introductions ("在下 X") become "My name is X".

Examples:
- 貧道 will disable his martial arts → I will disable his martial arts
- 孩兒 pays his respects → I pay my respects
- 在下 Li Gongming → My name is Li Gongming
- 弟子 would not dare → I would not dare

---

## Rule 4 — Common nouns → English
Generic martial-world and cultural nouns are translated. Technique names that are descriptive are translated; uniquely named techniques with no clean English equivalent stay in Chinese.

Examples:
- 武功 → martial arts / martial skill
- 功力 → inner strength
- 比武招親 / 擂台比武招親 → martial contest for her hand in marriage
- 出家人 → man of the cloth
- 還俗 → return to lay life
- 三聘六禮 → proper betrothal rites
- 繡球 → silk ball

---

## Rule 5 — Culturally specific terms → nuanced English
Terms with no single-word English equivalent are translated with care to preserve their specific nuance, not flattened to generic equivalents.

Examples:
- 登徒浪子 → lecherous good-for-nothing (a man who pursues women shamelessly)
- 苦衷 → the impossible bind I'm in / reasons I cannot easily explain (inexpressible inner hardship forcing a difficult choice)
- 名份 → the formal station that the world demands (social legitimacy required of a union)
- 得天獨厚 → heaven's favourite (blessed with gifts not of one's own making)

---

## Rule 6 — Idioms → English
Chinese idioms and set phrases are rendered as natural English equivalents, not translated literally or left in pinyin.

Examples:
- 調虎離山之計 → a ruse to lure me away
- 一石二鳥 / 一石二卵 → kills two birds with one stone
- 得天獨厚 → heaven's favourite

---

## Rule 7 — Restore nuance missing from English, preferring Cantonese
Where the Cantonese or Mandarin contains meaning, tone, or cultural detail absent from the English source, revise or expand the English line to include it. Cantonese is preferred where the two versions differ in nuance, word choice, or tone — use the Mandarin only to resolve ambiguity or supply detail absent from the Cantonese. Light copy-editing for grammar and fluency throughout.

Examples:
- "you're going to be in deep trouble" → "you'll be cast into the oil-cauldron of hell!"
- "Sometimes that's how it is when you work for the prince" → "Working for 王爺, you sometimes have to know your limits"

---

## Always kept in Chinese characters
- Personal names (人名)
- Place names (地名)
- Sect / org names (幫派)
- Named techniques (招式名)
- Epithets (稱號) e.g. 東邪, 西毒
- Honorific address forms (稱謂)
- Pet names / diminutives (暱稱)
- 江湖 (as the setting/world)
